Case study

The Joyful Cherry

Designing a self-service booking platform for a boutique Japan tour company — turning Instagram DMs into an end-to-end digital experience.

Product Designer
1 month
Web & Mobile
Figma

The problem

A great tour business hidden behind Instagram DMs

The Joyful Cherry is a boutique, founder-led tour business based in Tokyo, offering off-the-beaten-path experiences for international travelers. The founder, Kayo, relied entirely on Instagram DMs and WhatsApp to manage bookings — requiring constant manual back-and-forth across time zones.

Users had no way to discover tour details, compare options, or understand pricing without messaging Kayo directly. Every booking was a multi-day conversation. The lack of a professional presence reduced trust with potential customers and limited the business's ability to scale.

User pain points

No centralized place to browse tours or compare options. Had to message the founder for basic information like dates, prices, and itineraries. Unclear what was included, creating hesitation before booking.

Founder pain points

Repetitive questions consuming hours each week. Manual coordination across time zones. No professional web presence to build credibility or attract new customers organically.

Approach & research

Studying what great tour booking looks like

I began by researching competitors in the boutique travel and cultural tourism space — including Booking.com, Trip.com, and Japan-specific platforms like Wabunka — to identify best practices for tour booking experiences.

Key patterns emerged: successful platforms prioritized visual storytelling with high-quality imagery, provided clear tour details upfront (duration, location, inclusions), and built trust through customer testimonials and guide introductions. I mapped Kayo's existing Instagram/WhatsApp flow to identify exactly where friction occurred.

Competitive analysis findings

Top performers used immersive hero imagery, scannable tour detail layouts, prominent social proof, and frictionless CTAs. Personal connection with the guide was a key trust signal in the boutique segment.

Design principles I set

Answer common questions proactively. Highlight the unique value of authentic, off-the-beaten-path tours. Preserve the personal, boutique feel of Kayo's original WhatsApp experience while removing the friction.

Impact

From multi-day conversations to minutes

What I learned

Design for both sides of the marketplace

  • Understand founder constraints, not just user needs. The solution needed to work for both travelers seeking authentic experiences and a solo founder managing everything. A beautiful booking interface means nothing if the founder is still overwhelmed with admin work.

  • Good design for small businesses is about strategic automation. Not about adding features — about automating what's repetitive while preserving what makes the service special. Efficiency without losing the personal, curated feel that differentiates Joyful Cherry.

  • Trust is a design problem. Since customers are making significant financial and time commitments, the design needed to provide confidence through social proof, transparent information, and personal connection with the founder.

  • Typography and spacing are not polish — they are credibility. Feedback from my mentor highlighted how inconsistent line heights and unclear type hierarchy can undermine an otherwise strong concept. These details are what separate good from very good work.
